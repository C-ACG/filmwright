# Direction · Blocking & Coverage · 场面调度 · 轴线 · 覆盖 · 转场 · 剪辑节奏

> Where the actors go, where the camera goes to catch them, how the pieces cut
> together, and how scenes join. The grammar of the cut.
> 演员往哪走、镜头从哪拍、片段如何剪在一起、场与场如何衔接。

---

## §1. Blocking · 场面调度

Blocking is the choreography of actors *and* camera in space. It is the first
directorial decision — staging is storytelling before any lens is chosen.

- **Geography first.** Establish where everyone is and how the space works (often
  the opening wide of a scene), so every later tight shot reads in context.
- **Movement is motivated.** A character crosses the room because they want
  something there or are avoiding something here — not to "create energy."
- **Power through position.** Standing over a seated rival, blocking an exit,
  putting a desk between two people — physical staging carries the scene's subtext.
  Re-block mid-scene to mark a power shift (the one who was cornered now stands).
- **Depth staging.** Push secondary action into the background; let the foreground
  and background comment on each other in one frame, sparing a cut.
- **Choreograph for the camera.** Decide actor paths and camera paths together — a
  Steadicam follow only works if the blocking gives it a clean line to travel.

---

## §2. The 180° line · 轴线

Draw an imaginary line through the two main subjects (or along a character's line
of action/travel). **Keep the camera on one side of it.** Crossing the line
unmotivated flips screen direction — two people talking suddenly seem to face the
same way; a character running left now runs right — and the audience loses
orientation.

- Each character keeps a consistent screen side; eyelines match across the cut.
- To legitimately cross: move the camera across on-screen (a tracking move that
  re-establishes), cut on an insert/neutral shot, or have a character physically
  reposition. **Cross the line on purpose to disorient** (a fight, a breakdown) —
  but know you are doing it.

## §3. Coverage · 镜头覆盖

The set of shots that let an editor build the scene with rhythm and options.

**Standard conversation coverage 经典对话覆盖:**
1. **Master 全景主镜头** — the whole scene, wide, end to end. The safety net and the
   geography reference.
2. **OTS A / OTS B 双过肩** — over each shoulder; the spine of dialogue cutting.
3. **Singles / clean CU A / B 单人特写** — each face alone, for the moments a reaction
   must stand by itself.
4. **Inserts 插入** — the hand, the phone, the object the scene turns on.
5. **Reaction shots 反应镜头** — often the most important: the listener, not the speaker,
   tells the audience how to feel.

**Coverage discipline:**
- Shoot only what the cut needs — a focused director plans the edit, not "every
  angle in case." Decide the key shots from the beat's emotional logic.
- **Crossing the cut on motion** hides the cut; cutting on a static frame exposes it.
- **The reverse must match** — eyelines, screen direction, and lighting continuity
  across A and B.
- For micro-drama and fast formats, coverage compresses to a master + a punch-in
  CU on the payoff; you are designing for speed, not options.

## §4. Transitions · 转场

How one scene becomes the next. The transition is a thought, not just a join.

| Transition | 中文 | Reads as |
|---|---|---|
| Hard cut | 硬切 | the default; neutral, fast, invisible |
| Match cut | 匹配剪辑 | a shape/motion/sound carries across → links two ideas (the bone → the satellite) |
| Cutaway / cut-in | 切出 / 切入 | step to a detail or a parallel, then return |
| Dissolve | 叠化 | passage of time, dream, memory, softness |
| Fade in/out | 淡入 / 淡出 | a chapter opens or closes; let the audience breathe |
| J-cut / L-cut | 声画错位 | sound leads or trails the picture → smooth, lifelike seams; the next scene's audio starts under this one |
| Smash cut | 急切 | a jarring jump for shock or comedy |
| Whip / swish | 甩切 | kinetic energy across the join |

**Principle:** never let a transition *replace* story progress. A dissolve does not
fix a scene that didn't end on a turn. The strongest seams are **motivated** — a
sound bridge (J-cut) that ties a character's last word to the next scene's image,
a match cut that makes an argument the cut itself is making.

## §5. Editing rhythm · 剪辑节奏

The cut is the third actor. Shot duration and cutting pace control the audience's
pulse independent of content.

- **Pace by tension.** Tighten cuts (shorter shots) to raise heat — confrontation,
  chase, climax. Let shots run long to hold calm, dread, or intimacy.
- **Cut on action / on the beat.** Cutting mid-motion hides the cut and keeps energy;
  cutting on a line-end or a look directs meaning.
- **The held shot is a tool.** A shot held a beat too long creates discomfort or
  weight; the cut that *doesn't* come is its own statement.
- **Silence after density.** After a high-density sequence, a sudden still long take
  (and/or silence) is often the real climax — the editing equivalent of dual-track
  mismatch (`00-core-craft.md` §5).
- **Montage** compresses time and stacks meaning — borrow the short-film density
  technique for time-jumps and "trying things" passages.

## §6. Sound design intent · 声音设计（意图）

Not a mix sheet — the *intent* the breakdown should carry, since sound is half the
direction.

- **Diegetic vs non-diegetic 有源 / 无源** — sound from inside the world (a radio, a
  door) vs score/VO over it. Crossing between them (a character "hears" the score)
  is a deliberate device.
- **Sound bridge 声桥** — see J/L-cuts; audio is the smoothest transition tool.
- **Silence 静默** — the most underused. Strip sound at a peak to force the eye and
  manufacture a vacuum.
- **Off-screen sound 画外音** — what we hear but don't see expands the world and builds
  dread cheaply (the violence implied, never shown — also the compliance-safe path).
- **Score as subtext** — score that contradicts the image (cheerful music over
  catastrophe) is irony; score that matches is reinforcement. Choose which.

## §7. Coverage self-check · 调度自检

- [ ] Is the scene's geography established before the tight shots?
- [ ] Does the camera stay on one side of the 180° line (or cross it on purpose)?
- [ ] Do eyelines and screen direction match across reverses?
- [ ] Is every camera move and every cut motivated by the beat?
- [ ] Is there a reaction shot where the *listener* should carry the moment?
- [ ] Does the transition into and out of the scene make a thought, not just a join?
- [ ] Could a tighter coverage list tell the same story? (Cut what the edit won't use.)
