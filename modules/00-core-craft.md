# Module 00 · Core Craft · 通用编剧方法论

> Loaded by every format. The craft that does not change whether you are writing
> a 90-second concept film or a 24-episode series.
> 所有格式共用的底层方法论。

---

## §0. A film is a cross-section of life · 电影是现实生活的横截面

The geological foundation of everything else. A story is **not** told from a
character's birth to death — it is a slice cut from a world that has been running
long before the first frame.

1. **The world predates the story.** Characters carry backstory (the *Ghost*); the
   era has context; relationships have history. The first page is not the
   character's first day — they walk into scene one carrying all of it. The writer
   must *know* the backstory even when the audience never sees it.
2. **Start in the middle.** Drop the audience into a world already in motion. A
   good opening cuts in at the point of maximum tension, not "from the beginning."
   The audience back-fills the cause from behavior — more gripping than exposition.
3. **The opening is an attitude.** Scene one defines how the whole film *looks at
   the world*, not its theme. Genre announces rhythm through dense action; art
   film announces style through precise texture. Either way it is among the most
   designed passages in the film.
4. **Build, then cut.** The workflow is therefore: build the full world (backstory,
   world rules, character whole) → then carve (choose where to start, what to show,
   what to hide). Not "what do I want to say" but "what exists in this world, and
   through which window do I let the audience look in."

## §1. Dramatic action — the atomic unit · 戏剧动作

**Definition:** `dramatic action = Goal + Conflict`. The goal must have urgency;
the conflict must directly oppose it.

Every scene holds at least one **micro dramatic action** (goal → obstacle →
result; result may be success, failure, or an unexpected turn). Micro actions
aggregate up into sequence-level actions, and those into the film's core action.

**Tests:** Is the goal reasonable and urgent? Is the goal–obstacle relationship
strong enough to drive the story? Is the conflict the engine, not a decoration?

---

## §2. Character — refuse paper dolls · 人物设计

**Want vs Need.**
- **Want (外在需求)** — the goal that drives action; the thing the character
  consciously pursues.
- **Need (内在欲望)** — the soul-gap the character is unaware of; usually the root
  cause of the Want.

The tension between Want and Need is the engine of the arc. By the end the
character either gets the Need (growth arc) or refuses it (tragic / anti-arc).

**Arc.** A change from state A to state B that cannot be undone. Short forms make
one jump; long forms run several *false changes* before the real one.

**Depth scaling.**

| Format | Depth |
|---|---|
| Ultrashort | functional archetype — no bio, no arc |
| Short | Want / Need / Arc + one contradiction |
| Feature | full bio + **Ghost** (前史创伤) + **Lie** (相信的谎言) + **Flaw** (性格缺陷) |
| Series | feature depth, but the arc is budgeted across episodes |

**The full chain (long form):**
`Ghost → Lie → Flaw → Want (the wrong pursuit) → trials → facing the Need →
the choice (cling to the Lie or embrace the Need) → new normal.`

**Contradiction.** A strong character has at least one layer of contradiction —
outer presentation vs inner truth (confident outside / insecure inside). This is
what gives subtext room to live.

## §3. Visual writing · 视听化写作

**Show, don't tell.** Only what the camera sees and the mic hears. If an emotion
cannot be conveyed through physical behavior, it does not belong on the page.
- ✗ "He finally understood his father's sacrifice."
- ✓ He stood a moment, then took off his coat and laid it over the sleeping man.

**Subtext.** Dialogue is an iceberg — the spoken line is the tip. Avoid
on-the-nose lines; use action, pause, evasion, topic-change to carry the real
meaning.
- ✗ "I'm scared of losing you."
- ✓ "Take the coat when you go. … It's cold out."

**Action is subtext.** Every emotional beat must be photographable. A good script,
with all dialogue removed, still reads roughly true.

**Dialogue style.** Spoken, not written; each character a distinct register (word
choice, sentence length, verbal tics); no over-metaphor or bookish AI-prose; never
let characters tell each other what both already know.

## §4. The opening hook (all formats) · 开场钩子

Every film, every episode, every short must grab the audience at the open. The
hook's job is not to "set up background" — it is to make the audience unable to
look away before they even know what the story is.

A hook is **not** an explosion. Quiet can hook: a too-quiet image, a too-ordinary
detail that prompts "wait — what?" is a valid hook.

- **Genre / commercial:** a dense, high-tension excellent scene that simultaneously
  states the character (skill, nature, situation) and the genre key.
- **Art / character-driven:** a passage that represents the film's whole style and
  mood — distinct rather than tense.
- **Short:** the first 15 seconds decide life or death; the most effective open is
  an ordinary image with one detail that is *off*.
- **Ultrashort:** the open *is* the concept; 5 seconds to make the rule legible or
  the curiosity unbearable.
- **Series:** every episode needs one; the cold open (before the title) is the key
  position. Episode one's open does the heaviest lifting — world, character, genre,
  and the seed of the season's conflict, all at once.

**Self-check:** first image has visual punch or mystery? a concrete question forms
within 30s? state and tension are built *together*, not "set up, then start"? the
open's genre key matches the whole? pulled out alone, is the open worth watching?

## §5. Dual-track pacing · 双轨节奏

Pacing is two parallel tracks; they can sync or deliberately mismatch, and the
mismatch is itself a strong device.

**External plot rhythm** — density and intensity of events.
- Tight: dense events, fast cuts, multi-party dialogue, chase, confrontation, reveal.
- Loose: routine, solitude, transition, landscape, silence.

**Internal emotional rhythm** — the swing of the character's emotional state.
- Heavy: confession, betrayal, loss, fear, ecstasy, despair.
- Light: banter, small talk, quiet companionship.

The strongest scenes often **mismatch**:
- Plot loose + emotion heavy → two people eat in silence, nothing happens, but one
  of them leaves tomorrow. The calmer the frame, the heavier the feeling.
- Plot tight + emotion light → a frantic chase scored to a breezy pop song.

**Rules:** alternate tension and breath — sustained tension numbs, sustained slack
loses the audience. After every high-intensity stretch, give a beat to digest.
Avoid both tracks low for long (nothing to watch, nothing to feel) and both high
for long (fatigue, the climax stops being special). In the outline and breakdown,
sketch a **pacing wave** marking each beat's two intensities.

## §6. Runtime estimation · 时长估算

Every dramatic action and scene gets a runtime estimate — not just "how long" but
"how much of the film's rhythm it occupies."

- **Dialogue:** 1 standard page ≈ 1 minute on screen; in Chinese, ~200–250 字 of
  dialogue + action ≈ 1 minute.
- **Action / montage:** by image count (~3–5 s per discrete image).
- **Silence / emotion:** easy to underestimate — a character standing still may
  need 10–15 s on screen to carry its weight.
- **Transitions:** count them, ~2–5 s each.

**Why it matters:** a beat that reads important in the outline but runs 30 s gets
compressed to nothing on screen; a beat that eats 15% of runtime for a minor info
drop is dead weight. Only on paper, with runtimes marked, can you *see* where it
rushes, drags, or under-builds the climax.

**Rough budget (reference, adjust knowingly):**

| Format | Setup | Dev (first) | Dev (second) | Climax | Resolution |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Short (8 min) | 1.5 | 2 | 2 | 1.5 | 1 |
| Feature (100 min) | 25 | 30 | 25 | 15 | 5 |
| Series episode (25 min) | 4 | 10 | 6 | 3 | 2 |

## §7. Memory checkpoint · 记忆检查点（长内容防丢失）

Long-form work overflows the context window; setups, states, and arcs get
"forgotten," causing contradictions. A checkpoint is a structured snapshot — not a
recap — recording only what later writing must know. Template:
`templates/memory-checkpoint.md`.

**When to trigger:** short (after breakdown, once) · feature (each sequence) ·
series (each episode — most important; mid-episode if 6+ scenes). Also on:
resuming in a new session, details starting to blur, or a user "summarize progress"
request.

**Compression principles:** record conclusions not process; changes not constants;
only what downstream needs. **Active threads and unpaid setups are the most
important — list every one in every checkpoint**, since they are the first to be
forgotten.

## §8. Cross-format technique borrowing · 跨格式技法借鉴

Techniques are not siloed. Borrow the *thinking*, not the format.

- **Concept-film twist → any single scene.** When a scene needs punch, ask: can its
  last second make the audience re-read its first? (Feature inciting incident,
  series cold open / act-out, short ending.)
- **Concept-purity test → any logline.** "One sentence, photographable in 5s." If a
  feature can't be said in one sentence, the core isn't sharp enough.
- **McKee value-charge → every scene, any format.** "Did a core value flip between
  the scene's start and end?" If nothing changed, consider cutting it.
- **Ghost/Lie/Flaw → simplified into a short's character.** One hinted detail of an
  unspoken reason is enough to lift a flat character.
- **Series act-out logic → feature sequence seams.** End each sequence on a small
  hook; more pull than a plain "and then."
- **Short's density montage → feature/series time-jumps.** Stacked visual slices
  move faster than telling each story.

Use only when a concrete problem appears; it is most useful in the script-doctor
stage, when something feels wrong but you can't name where.
